## The shift

**Proportional share / fair share scheduling** tries to guarantee that each job gets a certain **percentage of CPU time**.

We're moving away from optimizing turnaround time and response time.

## Lottery scheduling (baseline)

- Each process gets **tickets** representing its share of the resource
- The scheduler picks a winning ticket at random — that process runs
- **Probabilistic, not deterministic** in the short term

### Ticket currency

Users with a set of tickets can allocate them among their own jobs. This converts into correct **global** ticket values.

Example: users A and B each get 100 global tickets.

- A's internal currency: 1000 tickets, 2 jobs → 500 internal each → **50 global** for A1, **50 global** for A2
- B's internal currency: 10 tickets, 1 job → **100 global** for B's job

### Ticket transfer

A process can temporarily hand off tickets to another process.

Good for **client/server** setups: the client passes tickets to the server to max out performance, then gets them back when the work finishes.

### Ticket inflation

A process can temporarily raise or lower the number of tickets it owns.

Works when groups of processes **trust** one another.

## Implementation

You need:

- A good random number generator
- A data structure to track processes and total tickets
- Loop through to find the process whose ticket counter exceeds the winner (heaps help for efficiency)

**Unfairness metric** = (time 1st job completes) / (time 2nd job completes), assuming both have the same number of tickets.

**How to assign tickets to jobs?** Open problem.

## Stride scheduling

**Deterministic** fair-share — combats the short-term randomness of lottery scheduling.

- **Stride** ∝ 1 / tickets
- Each time a process runs, its **pass** increases by its stride
- The process with the **lowest pass** runs next

**Downside:** global state.
