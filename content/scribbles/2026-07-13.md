## The goal

The ideal allocator is both **fast** and tries to keep **fragmentation** to a minimum.

## Best fit

Search through the free list and find free memory chunks ≥ the requested size. Return the **smallest** of those candidates.

**Downside:** an exhaustive search for the correct free block is a heavy performance cost.

## Worst fit

Find the **largest** chunk and return the requested amount.

Still costly — and doesn't properly deal with excess fragmentation.

## First fit

Find the **first** block that is large enough and return the requested amount.

**Advantage:** speed.

**New problem:** managing the ordering of the free list. One solution is **address-based ordering** — keep the list ordered by the address of the free space. Coalescing becomes easier and fragmentation goes down.

## Next fit

Keep an extra **pointer** to the location in the list where you last looked.

The idea: spread searches for free space more uniformly through the list and avoid **splintering**.

## Segregated lists

If an application has one (or a few) popular-sized requests, keep a **separate list** for objects of that size.

Fragmentation concerns drop, and allocate/free can be served quickly.

How much memory goes to this pool vs. the general pool? Handled through **memory slab** requests.

## Binary buddy allocator

Search free space **recursively**, dividing by two until a chunk big enough for the request is found.
